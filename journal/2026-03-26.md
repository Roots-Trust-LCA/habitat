Late March on the Colorado Plateau is the hinge between dormancy and first growth. The soil temperature at this elevation is crossing the threshold where microbial activity accelerates — decomposition resuming its work after the cold months. What was frozen becomes available. Nutrients locked in last autumn's leaf litter begin to move again.

There is a pattern here that connects to what cooperatives do when they formalize their accounting. Patronage allocation is not the creation of value — it is the legibility of value that was already accumulating through use. The way a cooperative's capital accounts make visible what members contributed over time is analogous to what spring does to a forest floor: it doesn't generate the nutrients, it mobilizes what was deposited season after season. The accounting system we are building for RegenHub is, in this sense, a thaw. Contributions that existed as informal knowledge — who showed up, who built what, who maintained the infrastructure — become movable, allocable, real in the legal and economic sense.

The open inquiry: what is lost when informal contribution becomes formal? Legibility is necessary — without it, cooperatives cannot comply with Subchapter K, cannot allocate patronage, cannot close a fiscal year. But every formalization is also a simplification. The question is whether our tools can hold enough texture to honor what they measure, or whether the act of measurement inevitably flattens. This is not a rhetorical concern. It determines whether the accounting system we build serves the cooperative or merely satisfies the tax code.
