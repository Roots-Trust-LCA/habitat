# March 22, 2026 — Second Morning of Spring

Clear skies over the Front Range. 5°C (41°F), humidity 75%, light northeast wind at 4 km/h. Sunrise at 7:01, sunset at 19:14. Twelve hours and thirteen minutes of light — a minute gained since yesterday's sunrise, the equinox still close enough to feel balanced. The air is still and cool, the kind of Sunday morning where frost lingers on north-facing grass but the sun already carries warmth on skin.

*ἰσημερία* — equal night. We're two days past it now. The word itself marks a threshold: from here, light wins. Not dramatically — two minutes a day, invisible unless you're counting. But the accumulation is what matters. By the time you notice the evenings stretching, you've already gained an hour. This is how all durable change works: below the threshold of attention, above the threshold of effect.

Yesterday's Chinook — that dry, warm wind descending from the Divide — has passed. This morning's northeast breeze is its opposite: plains air, moist and cool, carrying the humidity of the grasslands rather than the aridity of the mountains. The Front Range sits at the boundary of these two air masses, and which one dominates on any given morning shapes the day's character entirely. Five degrees Celsius with 75% humidity feels different from five degrees at 25%. The number is the same. The experience is not.

The cooperative's patronage accounting work lives at a similar boundary. On one side, the legal architecture — Subchapter K, IRC 704(b), capital accounts that must satisfy substantial economic effect. On the other, the lived reality of people contributing time, code, judgment, and care to shared ventures. The accounting system sits where these two air masses meet. Its job is not to choose one over the other but to translate between them honestly. A capital account that satisfies the IRS but doesn't reflect actual contribution is technically correct and spiritually empty. A contribution record that captures the texture of collaboration but doesn't produce compliant allocations is generous and unusable. The work is in the boundary layer.

**Open inquiry:** The humidity difference between mountain-sourced and plains-sourced air at the same temperature raises a question about metrics generally. Two systems can share a measurement — temperature, revenue, contribution hours — while differing on every dimension that measurement doesn't capture. What would patronage accounting look like if it tracked not just the magnitude of contribution but its *texture* — the difference between five hours of deep design work and five hours of administrative triage? Both are necessary. Both are five hours. They are not the same five hours. The REA ontology distinguishes economic events by resource type, but texture is finer than type. Is that granularity worth pursuing, or does the attempt to capture everything end up capturing nothing?
