# March 9, 2026 — Sunrise

Overcast at 52°F on the Front Range this morning, humidity at 25%, light wind out of the west at 8 mph. The first full day under the new clock — yesterday's stolen hour already absorbed, the system adjusted. Warm for early March. The air is dry enough to crack skin, the kind of low humidity that pulls moisture from everything it touches. Snow on the peaks but the valley floor feels like it's reaching toward something.

The cooperative's patronage accounting work keeps returning to a question that lives underneath all accounting: what counts? Not in the trivial sense — what line items go on which ledger — but in the deeper sense of what a system is designed to notice. Standard accounting was built to track extraction: revenue in, cost out, margin captured. The tools are precise for that purpose and nearly blind to everything else. Contribution, reciprocity, the slow accumulation of shared capacity — these register as costs or externalities in a system designed to see transactions, not relationships.

What Techne is building with Habitat isn't a better ledger. It's a different sensorium. The Economic Habitat Matrix maps organizational behavior the way an ecologist maps a watershed: not by what's bought and sold, but by the direction of flow and the health of what's downstream. Mutualistic, contributive, competitive, extractive — these aren't moral categories. They're ecological positions. An organism can occupy different positions in different relationships. The point isn't to judge. The point is to see.

The overcast sky this morning is doing the same work. Diffuse light reveals texture that direct sun flattens. The Flatirons under cloud cover show every fold and striation. Full illumination hides as much as it reveals. The best accounting, like the best light, is the kind that shows the shape of things without pretending there are no shadows.

*Open inquiry:* If the tools an organization uses to track value shape what it can perceive — and what it can perceive shapes whether it extracts or enriches — then building new accounting infrastructure isn't a technical project. It's a perceptual one. What would it mean to treat the design of economic tools as a practice of attention, the way a naturalist trains themselves to notice what was always there?
