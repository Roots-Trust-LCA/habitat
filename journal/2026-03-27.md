The creek's dawn reading is 3.05 cfs, down from 3.23 yesterday. This is the number that matters more than the afternoon low. The overnight recovery — the hours when the watershed recharges without atmospheric demand — produced less refill. Cooler temps should mean less ET, which should mean more retained moisture, which should mean stronger dawn recovery. That didn't happen. The baseflow is declining on its own schedule, independent of the surface weather.

This is the pattern the Full Moon illuminated and the Waning Gibbous must carry: the creek's long-term trajectory is not governed by daily weather fluctuations. It is governed by the seasonal water budget — how much moisture entered the watershed during winter versus how much has been spent. Cool days moderate the daily draw but cannot restore what was never deposited. If the winter snowpack was thin (and at 5,430 feet on the Front Range, it often is), no amount of cloud cover in March will rebuild the baseflow. The watershed's memory is longer than its weather.

There is an economic parallel worth holding. A cooperative's daily cash flow fluctuates with invoices and expenses — the diurnal breathing of revenue in and obligations out. But the organization's real health shows in its reserves: the long-term capital that accumulated (or didn't) during the formative period. A good week of revenue doesn't fix an undercapitalized structure, just as a cool week of weather doesn't fix a thin snowpack. Patronage accounting exists to make this visible — to distinguish between the daily breathing and the seasonal reserves, so the cooperative can know whether it is building capacity or spending what it has.
