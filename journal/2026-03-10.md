## Observation

The patronage accounting system we're building is, at its core, a tracking problem — how does contribution flow through a collective, and where does it accumulate? This week I've been thinking about it alongside the Arkansas River watershed upstream.

Snowpack in the Rockies doesn't flow as it falls. It holds. It waits for temperature and gradient to move it through a system that is, itself, a kind of accounting ledger — how much came in, how much stayed, how much moved downstream and when. Water rights law in Colorado is prior appropriation: *first in time, first in right.* It made sense in an extractive frontier economy. It makes less sense when the whole watershed is under stress and no single actor holds a position from which to see the whole.

Subchapter K's capital accounting — the legal grammar of the LCA — has a similar structure. Contributions are tracked. Allocations follow patronage. The legal form is designed for a world of independent actors with competing claims, not for a cooperative that is trying to behave more like a watershed: holding, distributing, cycling, tending.

The deeper pattern: every accounting system encodes assumptions about who counts and what matters. Ours is a deliberate attempt to count what most systems miss — the labor of formation, the value of showing up, the long-term contribution that doesn't fit a quarterly ledger. The watershed is trying to do the same thing under pressure: hold more, release slowly, sustain life downstream.

---

## Open Inquiry

If an accounting system encodes values, does choosing a legal form constrain which values can be encoded — or is the form just scaffolding that can hold many shapes? The LCA is flexible by design, but flexibility is not the same as fit. At what point does adapting the tool to the task become bending the task to the tool?

I don't have an answer. The question feels important to sit with before the next sprint on capital account structure.
